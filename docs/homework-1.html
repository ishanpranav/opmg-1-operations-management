Suppose that 450 visitors enter a carnival every hour. The visitors spend 90 minutes waiting in line for and going on the rides and then another 30 minutes eating. How many visitors are there at any given point in time at the carnival?

Question 3

At the doctor’s office there are 12 check-in machines and expect that a patient will spend 15 minutes checking in before proceeding to the main waiting room. If a patient will spend on average 20 minutes in the waiting room, what is the maximum number of seats that the doctor’s office should install in the waiting room?

We can apply Little's law for each process.

For the check-in process, let L1L_1L1​ denote the maximum inventory, λ1\lambda_1λ1​ denote the capacity, and w1w_1w1​ denote the average wait time.

L1=λ1w1,L_1=\lambda_1w_1, L1​=λ1​w1​,

12 patients=λ1⋅15 min,12\text{ patients}=\lambda_1\cdot15\text{ min}, 12 patients=λ1​⋅15 min,

λ1=12 patients15 min.\lambda_1=\frac{12\text{ patients}}{15\text{ min}}. λ1​=15 min12 patients​.

For the waiting process, let L2L_2L2​ denote the maximum inventory, λ2\lambda_2λ2​ denote the capacity, and w2w_2w2​ denote the average wait time. The capacity is limited by the capacity of the check-in process, so λ2=λ1\lambda_2=\lambda_1λ2​=λ1​.

L2=λ1w2,L_2=\lambda_1w_2, L2​=λ1​w2​,

L2=12 patients15 min⋅20 min,L_2=\frac{12\text{ patients}}{15\text{ min}}\cdot 20\text{ min}, L2​=15 min12 patients​⋅20 min,

L2=16 patients.L_2=16\text{ patients}. L2​=16 patients.

Thus, the doctor's office should install a maximum of 16 seats.
